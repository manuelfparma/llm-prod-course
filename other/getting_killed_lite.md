***Getting Killed*** is the fourth[[a]](#cite_note-2) studio album by American rock band [Geese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geese_%28band%29 "Geese (band)"), released on September 26, 2025, on [Partisan Records](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partisan_Records "Partisan Records") and [Play It Again Sam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PIAS_Recordings "PIAS Recordings").
The album marked the band's first release as a quartet following the
departure of guitarist Foster Hudson in late 2023 to pursue academia. It
was recorded in January 2025, one month after the release of frontman [Cameron Winter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cameron_Winter "Cameron Winter")'s debut solo album, *[Heavy Metal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heavy_Metal_%28album%29 "Heavy Metal (album)")* (2024), at producer [Kenny Beats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenny_Beats "Kenny Beats")' Putnam Hill studio in Los Angeles.

Musically, it is categorised as an [art rock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Art_rock "Art rock"), [indie rock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indie_rock "Indie rock"), and [experimental rock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experimental_rock "Experimental rock")
album. The album was shaped by extended jam sessions and an emphasis on
rhythmic repetition, abrasive textures, and unconventional song
structures. Winter's vocal performance is a prominent feature, ranging
from restrained [crooning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crooning "Crooning")
to strained, expressive outbursts, while the lyrics employ surreal
imagery and recurring themes of violence, power, and transformation.

The album was preceded by the singles "[Taxes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxes_%28song%29 "Taxes (song)")", "[Trinidad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trinidad_%28song%29 "Trinidad (song)")", and "[100 Horses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/100_Horses "100 Horses")", and further supported by the singles "Cobra" and "[Au Pays du Cocaine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Au_Pays_du_Cocaine "Au Pays du Cocaine")", and a [headlining tour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Getting_Killed_World_Tour "Getting Killed World Tour")
beginning in October 2025. It received critical acclaim, and was named
among the best albums of 2025 by several publications, with *[The New Yorker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_New_Yorker "The New Yorker")* and *[Stereogum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stereogum "Stereogum")* placing it at number one. Commercially, it charted at number 96 on the [*Billboard* 200](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Billboard_200 "Billboard 200"), and topped the [UK Independent Albums Chart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UK_Independent_Albums_Chart "UK Independent Albums Chart").

## Background

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Getting_Killed&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Background")]

Following the release of their third album *[3D Country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3D_Country "3D Country")* (2023), [Geese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geese_%28band%29 "Geese (band)") transitioned from a quintet to a quartet.[[2]](#cite_note-corcoran-nina-2025-3)
The departure of guitarist Foster Hudson was formally announced on 22
December 2023, attributed to his decision to pursue academic work,[[3]](#cite_note-4) leaving frontman [Cameron Winter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cameron_Winter "Cameron Winter"), guitarist [Emily Green](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emily_Green_%28musician%29 "Emily Green (musician)"), bassist Dominic DiGesu, and drummer [Max Bassin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Bassin "Max Bassin") as the band's core members.[[4]](#cite_note-brucejones-2025-5) With Hudson gone, Winter assumed a larger share of the guitar arrangements on the new material.[[5]](#cite_note-RS-6)
DiGesu and Green later described the lineup change as a motivating
factor that compelled them to refine their collaborative approach.[[6]](#cite_note-currin-2025-7)

Geese's collaboration with producer [Kenny Beats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenny_Beats "Kenny Beats"), a.k.a.
Kenneth Blume, began informally in mid-2024, when Blume encountered the
band's merchandise and became curious about their work. The two met at
the [Austin City Limits Music Festival](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austin_City_Limits_Music_Festival "Austin City Limits Music Festival")
in October 2024, where Blume expressed an enthusiasm for leaving
imperfections intact—an attitude Winter regarded as a rare and
liberating approach for a rock-adjacent producer.[[6]](#cite_note-currin-2025-7) Geese spent several days that November at Putnam Hill, Blume's newly built studio in Los Angeles, to develop preliminary [demos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demo_%28music%29 "Demo (music)").
Blume initially found the material structurally chaotic, though his
view shifted after hearing a solo project Winter released later that
year, which clarified the songs' internal logic and convinced him of the
project's potential.[[6]](#cite_note-currin-2025-7)

The release of Winter's solo album [*Heavy Metal*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heavy_Metal_%28album%29 "Heavy Metal (album)") on December 6, 2024[[7]](#cite_note-8) generated an unexpected impact of critical attention. Despite modest expectations from the label,[[6]](#cite_note-currin-2025-7) the album became a cult success[[8]](#cite_note-breihan-2025-9)[[9]](#cite_note-aroesti-2025-10) and was acclaimed for its songwriting and vocal experimentation.*[[10]](#cite_note-11)*
Its reception drew new attention to Geese's work and heightened
anticipation for their next release. He later stated that the success of
the solo album increased his confidence and informed his intention to
make *Getting Killed* a louder and more forceful record than its predecessor.[[11]](#cite_note-griffin-2025-12)

## Recording and production

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Getting_Killed&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Recording and production")]

[![](Getting%20Killed%20-%20Wikipedia_files/Kenny_Beats_at_Hard_Summer_2019_002.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File%3AKenny_Beats_at_Hard_Summer_2019.png)

*Getting Killed* was recorded and produced at [Kenny Beats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenny_Beats "Kenny Beats")' studio *(pictured)*

Recording took place in January 2025 at the Putnam Hill studio,[[5]](#cite_note-RS-6) and *Getting Killed* was produced by the band itself alongside [Kenny Beats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenny_Beats "Kenny Beats").[[12]](#cite_note-13) The overall process was recorded over a ten-day period, with the band working fourteen-hour days while the [Southern California wildfires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/January_2025_Southern_California_wildfires "January 2025 Southern California wildfires") filled the Los Angeles air with smoke and ash.[[6]](#cite_note-currin-2025-7)
Winter described the process as a "waking nightmare until it's
mastered", and the band arrived with around twenty demos that were often
incomplete, forcing them to write, restructure, and refine material in
real time.[[5]](#cite_note-RS-6) Much of the album emerged from extended thirty-minute jam sessions centered on repetitive grooves,[[5]](#cite_note-RS-6)[[13]](#cite_note-deville-2025-14) which Bassin characterized as explorations of patterns that "feel almost like they don't end".[[11]](#cite_note-griffin-2025-12)

Blume, who is known for his work in [hip-hop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hip-hop "Hip-hop"),[[14]](#cite_note-pitchforkreview-15) informed an emphasis on the rhythm section,[[15]](#cite_note-TLoBF-16) resulting in Bassin's drumming operating in what *[Under the Radar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Under_the_Radar_%28magazine%29 "Under the Radar (magazine)")* writer Mark Moody called "maximum overdrive"[[16]](#cite_note-moody-2025-17) and in bass and drum performances being tracked repeatedly until they could support the album's more experimental textures.[[6]](#cite_note-currin-2025-7)
The production also incorporated deliberately abrasive or
unconventional elements across the album. The group spent one session
selecting a single handclap sample from a folder of roughly 7,000
options, an example they later noted had briefly delayed completion of
the associated song. Winter's vocal recording process swung between
extremes, with some takes captured immediately and others requiring more
than forty attempts in order to push his voice into the desired
emotionally frayed state.[[5]](#cite_note-RS-6)

## Musical style

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Getting_Killed&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Musical style")]

### Overview

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Getting_Killed&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Overview")]

*Getting Killed* has been described by journalists as [art rock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Art_rock "Art rock"),[[17]](#cite_note-DIYreview-18)[[18]](#cite_note-allmusicGK-19)[[19]](#cite_note-20) [indie rock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indie_rock "Indie rock"),[[9]](#cite_note-aroesti-2025-10)[[20]](#cite_note-krueger-2025-21)[[21]](#cite_note-nmerev-22) and [experimental rock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experimental_rock "Experimental rock")[[4]](#cite_note-brucejones-2025-5)[[21]](#cite_note-nmerev-22) with additional elements and influences including [jazz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jazz "Jazz"),[[13]](#cite_note-deville-2025-14)[[22]](#cite_note-amen-2025-23) [no wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/No_wave "No wave")[[18]](#cite_note-allmusicGK-19)[[23]](#cite_note-exclaimrev-24) [funk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Funk "Funk"),[[13]](#cite_note-deville-2025-14)[[15]](#cite_note-TLoBF-16) [progressive rock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Progressive_rock "Progressive rock"),[[22]](#cite_note-amen-2025-23) [noise music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noise_music "Noise music"),[[13]](#cite_note-deville-2025-14) and [motorik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motorik "Motorik")-inspired rhythmic patterns.[[15]](#cite_note-TLoBF-16) The album consists of eleven tracks with a total running time of 45 minutes.[[24]](#cite_note-liner-25) The compositions rely on cyclical rhythmic figures,[[14]](#cite_note-pitchforkreview-15) recurrent [motifs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motif_%28music%29 "Motif (music)"), and definitive structures that unfold gradually rather than toward conventional hook-driven climaxes.[[22]](#cite_note-amen-2025-23) Winter's vocal performance has been described as a central element to the band's sound. His delivery ranges from soft baritone[[20]](#cite_note-krueger-2025-21) [crooning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crooning "Crooning")[[22]](#cite_note-amen-2025-23) to strained, cracking shouts,[[25]](#cite_note-hazelwood-2025-26) drawing occasional comparisons to [Thom Yorke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thom_Yorke "Thom Yorke") of [Radiohead](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiohead "Radiohead") and [Mick Jagger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mick_Jagger "Mick Jagger") of [the Rolling Stones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Rolling_Stones "The Rolling Stones").[[26]](#cite_note-mojo-27) The lyrics are dense and allusive, combining biblical imagery[[4]](#cite_note-brucejones-2025-5) and surreal or absurd scenarios.[[9]](#cite_note-aroesti-2025-10)

### Songs

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Getting_Killed&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Songs")]

The opening track "[Trinidad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trinidad_%28song%29 "Trinidad (song)")" establishes the album's volatile and chaotic tone[[13]](#cite_note-deville-2025-14)[[27]](#cite_note-slantreview-28), featuring the uncommon time signature of **13**
**8**.[[28]](#cite_note-Bennett-29) It features jagged guitars,[[27]](#cite_note-slantreview-28) explosive percussion,[[20]](#cite_note-krueger-2025-21) erratic bursts of brass,[[9]](#cite_note-aroesti-2025-10) and layers of discordant noise.[[25]](#cite_note-hazelwood-2025-26) Critics compared the sound to "Radiohead on [PCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phencyclidine "Phencyclidine")"[[29]](#cite_note-farrell-2025-30) or a funk ensemble pushed into [noise-rock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noise_rock "Noise rock") extremes.[[13]](#cite_note-deville-2025-14) Winter's vocals alternate between languid phrasing and sharp, shrieking outbursts.[[22]](#cite_note-amen-2025-23) The refrain, "There's a bomb in my car!", forms one of the album's most memorable lines,[[9]](#cite_note-aroesti-2025-10)[[14]](#cite_note-pitchforkreview-15) and the Australian musician [Nick Cave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nick_Cave "Nick Cave") remarked that the shift into the chorus creates a moment in which "all worry is laid to waste".[[30]](#cite_note-31)[[31]](#cite_note-32) "Cobra" introduces a more melodic and relaxed atmosphere.[[25]](#cite_note-hazelwood-2025-26)[[27]](#cite_note-slantreview-28) Its breezy textures recall a [Beach Boys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Beach_Boys "The Beach Boys")-adjacent palette,[[20]](#cite_note-krueger-2025-21) while Winter's vocals adopt a crooning tone that Holly Hazelwood of *Spectrum Culture* interpreted as evidence of his potential as a "neo-crooner".[[25]](#cite_note-hazelwood-2025-26) The song blends interlocking guitar melodies and nostalgia[[32]](#cite_note-mills-2025-33) with an attempt to subvert pop conventions.[[22]](#cite_note-amen-2025-23) "Husbands" is built on a steady, metronomic drum pattern and supple guitar lines reminiscent of the [John Frusciante](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Frusciante "John Frusciante") period of [Red Hot Chili Peppers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Hot_Chili_Peppers "Red Hot Chili Peppers"). It incorporates elements of [gospel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gospel_music "Gospel music")-inflected harmony, extended groove-based structure, and [R&B](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R%26B "R&B")-tinged [lo-fi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lo-fi_music "Lo-fi music") production.[[22]](#cite_note-amen-2025-23) The track conveys a sense of being burdened by a city for which there remains affection,[[32]](#cite_note-mills-2025-33) as reflected in Winter's line "I'll repeat what I say, but I'll never explain".[[33]](#cite_note-OMH-34)

The title track "Getting Killed" begins with an upbeat, crunchy rhythm before resolving into a more settled groove.[[22]](#cite_note-amen-2025-23) It features a [blues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blues "Blues")-derived guitar line and a sampled Ukrainian choral[[21]](#cite_note-nmerev-22) passage described by Rachel Aroesti of *[The Guardian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Guardian "The Guardian")* as a "frantic pile-up of voices and grooves".[[9]](#cite_note-aroesti-2025-10) Writing for *[Exclaim!](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exclaim%21 "Exclaim!")*, Eric Hill likened the song's character to a "[Julian Casablancas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julian_Casablancas "Julian Casablancas")-fronted [Black Crowes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Black_Crowes "The Black Crowes")".[[23]](#cite_note-exclaimrev-24) "Islands of Men" opens with a steady bass figure[[32]](#cite_note-mills-2025-33) and gradually develops its warm atmosphere over six minutes.[[20]](#cite_note-krueger-2025-21) The track has been described as swaying [boogie rock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boogie_rock "Boogie rock") that transitions into something more ethereal as it proceeds.[[15]](#cite_note-TLoBF-16) "[100 Horses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/100_Horses "100 Horses")", a rapid, high-energy piece[[8]](#cite_note-breihan-2025-9) built on repetitive, militant grooves,[[27]](#cite_note-slantreview-28) leans heavily into funk-inflected rhythm and percussive force.[[33]](#cite_note-OMH-34) Bassin's drumming dominates the arrangement,[[32]](#cite_note-mills-2025-33) and Winter's lyrics engage in a "pantomime of [American imperialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_imperialism "American imperialism")",[[4]](#cite_note-brucejones-2025-5) with lines such as "There is only [dance music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dance_music "Dance music")
in times of war" and "All people must die scared or else die nervous"
linking the track to the album's broader critique of violence and power.[[8]](#cite_note-breihan-2025-9)[[14]](#cite_note-pitchforkreview-15) "Half Real" evokes a [Tom Waits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tom_Waits "Tom Waits")-like preacherly tone[[16]](#cite_note-moody-2025-17) and draws on a bar-room atmosphere[[26]](#cite_note-mojo-27) marked by [drones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drone_%28sound%29 "Drone (sound)") reminiscent of [the Velvet Underground](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Velvet_Underground "The Velvet Underground")'s "[Venus in Furs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venus_in_Furs_%28song%29 "Venus in Furs (song)")".[[20]](#cite_note-krueger-2025-21)
Winter's vocal performance leans into a spoken-sung delivery as he
contemplates extreme personal erasure: "I've got half a mind, to just
pay for the lobotomy... I've got no more thinking to do".[[34]](#cite_note-pasterev-35)

Described by *[MusicOMH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MusicOMH "MusicOMH")*'s John Murphy as of the album's most subdued tracks,[[33]](#cite_note-OMH-34) "[Au Pays du Cocaine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Au_Pays_du_Cocaine "Au Pays du Cocaine")" adopts a gentle, swaying mood and highlights Winter's baritone,[[20]](#cite_note-krueger-2025-21) rendered in a manner akin to a [post-punk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Post-punk "Post-punk") crooner.[[35]](#cite_note-36) Its melodic structure recalls a [folk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Folk_music "Folk music") lullaby.[[34]](#cite_note-pasterev-35) The lyrics address a reluctant partner with lines such as "You can be free and still come home"[[16]](#cite_note-moody-2025-17) and "Baby you can change and still choose me".[[33]](#cite_note-OMH-34) "Bow Down" reintroduces a dense, energetic style, characterized by a sharp guitar-and-drum interlock[[22]](#cite_note-amen-2025-23) and a rhythmic language described by Murphy as "jerky [math-rock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Math_rock "Math rock")".[[33]](#cite_note-OMH-34) The track reshapes a [staccato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Staccato "Staccato") riff into an off-kilter dance figure.[[20]](#cite_note-krueger-2025-21)
Winter addresses transformation and shifting identity through metaphors
such as "I was a sailor, and now I'm a boat... I was a car, and now I'm
the road",[[13]](#cite_note-deville-2025-14) giving the song a chant-like quality that reinterprets elements of the band's earlier post-punk style.[[14]](#cite_note-pitchforkreview-15) "[Taxes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxes_%28song%29 "Taxes (song)")" is constructed around a polyrhythmic introduction that abruptly opens into wide, chiming guitar arrangements.[[26]](#cite_note-mojo-27) The track has been described as a cathartic rock anthem,[[32]](#cite_note-mills-2025-33) with Winter's vocals combining desperation and melodic clarity.[[36]](#cite_note-37)
The lyrics incorporate biblical references and bleak humor, exemplified
by "If you want me to pay my taxes / You'd better come over with a
crucifix / You're gonna have to nail me down".[[4]](#cite_note-brucejones-2025-5) "Long Island City Here I Come" builds in intensity and volume.[[25]](#cite_note-hazelwood-2025-26)[[33]](#cite_note-OMH-34) The final section features Winter's voice gradually receding[[32]](#cite_note-mills-2025-33) into a stream-of-consciousness monologue[[33]](#cite_note-OMH-34) involving images such as "Microphones hidden under your bed"[[15]](#cite_note-TLoBF-16) and apocalyptic anxieties.[[34]](#cite_note-pasterev-35)

## Artwork

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Getting_Killed&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Artwork")]

The cover features Green in a white dress, her appearance
being obfuscated by a lens flare. In her right hand is a trumpet held
closely to her face, while the left hand holds a revolver pointed
directly at the camera. The image was shot on photographer Mark
Sommerfeld's rooftop in [Ridgewood, Queens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ridgewood%2C_Queens "Ridgewood, Queens") with additional editing by Kyle Berger to position the sun correctly.[[37]](#cite_note-Gibson-38) The cover for *Getting Killed* has been compared to the cover for *[Vision Creation Newsun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vision_Creation_Newsun "Vision Creation Newsun")* by Japanese band [Boredoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boredoms "Boredoms").[[13]](#cite_note-deville-2025-14) Creative director Phil Gibson said that he was inspired by depictions of death in paintings by artists [Sascha Schneider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sascha_Schneider "Sascha Schneider") and [Thomas Cole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Cole "Thomas Cole"), as well as in movies like *[The Seventh Seal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Seventh_Seal "The Seventh Seal")*. The direction of the album art also came from playing around with the title of *Getting Killed*.

> The
> real fun was considering the perspective of the title ‘Getting Killed.’
> Who or what is getting killed? Who or what is doing the killing? Are we
> picking a side or leaving it to interpretation?

— Phil Gibson on conceptualizing the cover for *Getting Killed*, in an interview with *[The Fader](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Fader "The Fader")*.[[37]](#cite_note-Gibson-38)

## Release

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Getting_Killed&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Release")]

On June 23, 2025, the band announced that the band would
be embarking on a North American headlining tour in support of their
forthcoming fourth studio album. The tour began in [South Burlington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Burlington%2C_Vermont "South Burlington, Vermont"), Vermont on October 10, and concluded in [Brooklyn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brooklyn "Brooklyn"), New York on November 21. The album had not yet been formally titled at the time of the tour announcement;[[2]](#cite_note-corcoran-nina-2025-3) it was officially announced on 8 July 2025, when Geese revealed the title and track listing and released the lead single, "[Taxes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxes_%28song%29 "Taxes (song)")".
The song was accompanied by a music video featuring disorienting
imagery of moshing crowds and staged violence, reflecting the album's
abrasive and surreal tone.[[38]](#cite_note-hardman-neville-2025-39) On 24 July 2025, frontman Cameron Winter leaked the album's opening track, "[Trinidad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trinidad_%28song%29 "Trinidad (song)")", during an [Instagram Live](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instagram_Live "Instagram Live") stream filmed in [Newport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newport%2C_Rhode_Island "Newport, Rhode Island"),
Rhode Island. Winter stated that he wanted to deliver the song
"directly to the people", and the track was subsequently issued through
official channels shortly thereafter.[[29]](#cite_note-farrell-2025-30) The song was officially released five days later, on July 29.[[39]](#cite_note-40) The final single released ahead of the album was "[100 Horses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/100_Horses "100 Horses")", which arrived on 26 August 2025.[[8]](#cite_note-breihan-2025-9) Its release coincided with the announcement of the European leg of the [Getting Killed World Tour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Getting_Killed_World_Tour "Getting Killed World Tour"),
scheduled for March 2026. The tour includes dates across mainland
Europe, followed by five shows in the United Kingdom, including
performances in Bristol, Glasgow, Leeds, Manchester, and London.[[40]](#cite_note-41) Additional dates were added in London and Glasgow following strong ticket demand.[[41]](#cite_note-42) Geese premiered the song "Cobra" on [BBC Radio 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BBC_Radio_1 "BBC Radio 1") a day before the album's release on September 25, 2025, where host [Jack Saunders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jack_Saunders_%28presenter%29 "Jack Saunders (presenter)") labeled the song as the "Hottest Record" of the night.[[42]](#cite_note-43)

*Getting Killed* was released on September 26 through [Partisan Records](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partisan_Records "Partisan Records") and [Play It Again Sam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PIAS_Recordings "PIAS Recordings").[[38]](#cite_note-hardman-neville-2025-39) A day after the album's release, the band played a free show in [Greenpoint, Brooklyn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenpoint%2C_Brooklyn "Greenpoint, Brooklyn"), outside the headquarters of [The Lot Radio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Lot_Radio "The Lot Radio"). It was attended by thousands of people.[[43]](#cite_note-44) A music video for "[Au Pays du Cocaine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Au_Pays_du_Cocaine "Au Pays du Cocaine")" was released on October 8, which depicted Winter interacting with a baby in a surreal domestic scenario.[[44]](#cite_note-:0-45) Geese also supported the album with television and studio performances, including an appearance on *[Jimmy Kimmel Live!](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jimmy_Kimmel_Live%21 "Jimmy Kimmel Live!")* performing "Taxes".[[44]](#cite_note-:0-45) On November 20, the band became the first act in nearly three years to appear on the *[From the Basement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/From_the_Basement "From the Basement")* series run by record producer [Nigel Godrich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigel_Godrich "Nigel Godrich"), recording a 35-minute set drawn largely from the album.[[45]](#cite_note-46) Geese appeared as musical guests on *[Saturday Night Live](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live "Saturday Night Live")* on January 24, 2026, with their setlist including "Au Pays du Cocaine" and "Trinidad".[[46]](#cite_note-47)

*Getting Killed* has received acclaim from critics. At [Metacritic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metacritic "Metacritic"), which assigns a weighted average rating out of 100 to reviews from mainstream critics, *Getting Killed* received a rating of 89 out of 100 based on nineteen critic reviews, indicating "universal acclaim".[[48]](#cite_note-mc-49) Similarly, on AnyDecentMusic?, it received a rating of 8.7 out of 10, based on 22 reviews.[[47]](#cite_note-adm-48)

Sam Sodomsky, writing for *[Pitchfork](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pitchfork_%28website%29 "Pitchfork (website)")*, designated the album with its "Best New Music" tag, writing that *Getting Killed*
is Geese's "strangest and strongest work". He characterized the album
as "anxious, fragmented music" that rejects traditional rock structures
in favor of groove-driven repetition and abrupt emotional shifts, noting
that the band balances paranoia, tenderness, and humor while embracing
what he described as a "restless, untameable curiosity".[[14]](#cite_note-pitchforkreview-15) *[The Guardian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Guardian "The Guardian")*'s Rachel Aroesti gave the album four out of five stars and wrote that *Getting Killed* "can be opaque, but its brilliance is still obvious", praising its blend of sonic dissonance and surreal, sardonic lyricism.[[9]](#cite_note-aroesti-2025-10) Devin Birse, writing for *[The Line of Best Fit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Line_of_Best_Fit "The Line of Best Fit")*, wrote that *Getting Killed*
is "the work of a real band", describing it as "Geese at their most
honest and sincere" and arguing that it "cements them as no longer
excellent imitators, but worthy equals".[[15]](#cite_note-TLoBF-16) Writing for *[Beats Per Minute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beats_Per_Minute_%28website%29 "Beats Per Minute (website)")*, John Amen gave the album a "recommended" score, writing that, with *Getting Killed*,
"Geese radicalize the conventional, adopting recognizable approaches
but with that insurrectional flair so integral to their DNA".[[49]](#cite_note-50)

Rhian Daly from *[NME](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NME "NME")*, wrote that *Getting Killed*
is "another exhilaratingly free-spirited turn" for Geese, praising the
band's ability to balance "erratic but original" ideas while remaining
in control of the album's shifting textures.[[21]](#cite_note-nmerev-22) In a review published by *[Stereogum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stereogum "Stereogum")*,
Chris DeVille characterized Getting Killed as "an apocalyptic album for
apocalyptic times". The critic emphasized the album's unruly structure
and improvisatory drive, arguing that Geese push beyond conventional
rock forms while retaining a sharp sense of irony, ultimately casting
the band as "prospective messiahs for [Gen Z](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gen_Z "Gen Z") rock 'n' roll".[[13]](#cite_note-deville-2025-14) Eric Hill from *[Exclaim!](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exclaim%21 "Exclaim!")* praised Geese's musicianship and flair for classic rock pastiche while suggesting that *Getting Killed* feels more like stylistic experimentation than a fully realized artistic statement.[[23]](#cite_note-exclaimrev-24)
